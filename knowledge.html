Repairing Dry Spots and Improving Soil Moisture

Dry spots in turfgrass often develop when water does not penetrate uniformly across the lawn. Contributing factors include hydrophobic soils, compacted soil layers, clogged or poorly adjusted sprinkler heads, shallow root systems and dry, windy weather. To diagnose localized dry spots, probe the soil with a screwdriver or soil probe – if the soil is hard to penetrate or remains dry at a depth of a few inches while other areas are moist, the irrigation system or soil conditions are likely the cause.

Remediation tactics

  • Check irrigation coverage – Make sure sprinklers overlap properly and adjust nozzles so water reaches each area evenly. Clogged or mis-aligned heads can create wet and dry patterns.
  • Water deeply and infrequently – Experts recommend irrigating to a soil depth of 4–6 inches and allowing the turf to dry slightly between cycles【255466315847790†L89-L116】. Deep irrigation encourages roots to grow deeper and reduces localized dry spot. Use the cycle-and-soak method (multiple shorter irrigation cycles) to avoid runoff on clay soils.
  • Aerate and amend the soil – Compacted soils restrict water infiltration and oxygen, leading to shallow roots and dry patches. Core aeration creates holes that allow water and air into the root zone【944223463244220†L50-L99】. After aerating, topdress with compost to improve soil structure and increase water-holding capacity【255466315847790†L124-L139】.
  • Address hydrophobicity – In sandy or heavily thatched lawns, soil particles may repel water. Applying a wetting agent or lightly raking in compost can help water soak into the root zone.
  • Reduce traffic and thatch – Concentrated foot traffic compacts soil, and a thick thatch layer prevents water from reaching the soil. Minimize traffic in wet conditions, remove excess thatch, and incorporate organic matter to maintain a loose, well-aerated profile.

Identifying and Managing Turfgrass Diseases

Warm-season lawns in Central Texas can suffer from several fungal diseases when conditions are favorable. Proper cultural practices are the most effective defense against disease.

Brown Patch (Large Patch)

Brown patch (caused by the fungus *Rhizoctonia solani*) affects St. Augustinegrass, Bermudagrass and Zoysiagrass, especially during warm, humid weather in spring and fall. Symptoms include circular patches of brown turf with yellow or orange borders; affected leaves detach easily at the leaf sheath【416816922912814†L67-L88】.

Management: - Avoid applying high rates of nitrogen fertilizer in late fall【416816922912814†L67-L88】. - Water only when needed and early in the morning, applying enough to wet the soil 4–6 inches【416816922912814†L67-L88】. - Mow regularly at the recommended height for the species (see mowing section) and remove clippings in infected areas【416816922912814†L67-L88】. - Improve drainage, reduce thatch and soil compaction, and provide adequate sunlight to reduce humidity at the soil surface. - Fungicides labelled for brown patch can be used as a preventive treatment when conditions are favorable, but cultural practices are more important.

Take-All Root Rot

Take-all root rot (TARR) is a serious disease of St. Augustinegrass and other warm-season turfs. It attacks the roots and stolons, causing yellowing, thinning and eventual decline. Although details were not provided in our sources, Texas A&M recommends maintaining a slightly acidic soil pH (6.0–6.5), applying topdressings of compost, and avoiding excess irrigation or fertilization to manage TARR.

Other diseases

Gray leaf spot, dollar spot and rust can also occur in warm-season lawns. These fungi are usually favored by stress (drought, compaction) and excessive nitrogen. The same cultural practices used to prevent brown patch – proper mowing height, deep infrequent watering and adequate air circulation – will help reduce disease pressure.

Diagnosing Compacted Soil and Shallow Roots

Compacted soil is common in high-traffic lawns and clay soils. When soil particles are pressed together, large pores disappear, reducing oxygen and water infiltration. Compacted soil leads to shallow roots, poor turf growth and increased runoff【944223463244220†L50-L99】. Signs of compaction include standing water after rain or irrigation, thin or discolored turf, and difficulty inserting a screwdriver into the ground.

Remediation tactics

  • Core aeration – Removing small cores of soil from the turf increases air exchange and water penetration. For heavily trafficked turf, aerate multiple times per year【944223463244220†L50-L99】.
  • Compost topdressing – After aeration, spread a thin layer of compost over the lawn and rake it into the holes. This adds organic matter, improves soil structure, and encourages beneficial microbes【255466315847790†L124-L139】.
  • Reduce traffic – Keep vehicles and foot traffic off wet turf to avoid further compaction【944223463244220†L50-L99】. Create designated pathways or stepping stones.
  • Maintain adequate moisture – Compacted soils dry quickly. Water deeply and infrequently to encourage roots to explore deeper soil layers【255466315847790†L89-L116】.

Best Mowing Procedures

Proper mowing is critical for turf health. Mowing too short stresses the grass, reduces its root system, and makes it more susceptible to drought and weeds. Always follow the “one-third rule” – never remove more than one-third of the leaf blade at one mowing【255466315847790†L89-L116】. Sharpen mower blades regularly and mow when the grass is dry to avoid spreading disease. Recommended mowing heights for common Central Texas grasses are summarized below:

Best Watering Schedules

Efficient irrigation conserves water and promotes healthy turf. Central Texas typically receives 25–35 inches of rainfall annually, so supplemental irrigation is needed during drought or in sandy soils. Key points include:

  • Deep, infrequent watering – Irrigate to a depth of 4–6 inches each time so that the soil profile is thoroughly wetted【255466315847790†L89-L116】. Allow the soil to dry slightly between waterings to encourage roots to grow deeper.
  • Morning irrigation – Water early in the morning to minimize evaporation and reduce disease incidence【416816922912814†L67-L88】. Avoid evening watering, which leaves foliage wet overnight and encourages fungal growth.
  • Adjust for soil type – Clay soils hold water longer and require less frequent irrigation than sandy soils. On heavy clay, use the cycle-and-soak method (e.g., two 10-minute runs separated by an hour) to avoid runoff. Sandy soils may need shorter intervals but more frequent watering.
  • Use rain sensors and soil moisture probes – Modern irrigation controllers allow adjustment based on weather and soil moisture. Install a rain sensor to prevent watering during or after rainfall.
  • Dormancy – Warm-season grasses can survive moderate drought by going dormant and turning brown. Occasional irrigation during dormancy (0.5 inch every 2–3 weeks) will keep crowns alive until rain returns.

Typical Local Grass Species and Best Management Practices

Central Texas lawns use several warm-season turf species. Each species has strengths and weaknesses and different soil and water requirements.

St. Augustinegrass (Stenotaphrum secundatum)

Characteristics: Coarse, dark-green blades; spreads by stolons; shade-tolerant but not traffic tolerant.

Adaptation: Performs well in humid areas with at least 35 inches of annual rainfall; in Central Texas it requires supplemental irrigation【312468846210205†L140-L155】.

Mowing & water: Mow at 3–4 inches【993902573354858†L89-L114】. It has moderate drought tolerance and needs deep but less frequent watering; avoid over-watering to prevent diseases like brown patch【416816922912814†L67-L88】.

Soil preferences: Prefers fertile, well-drained soils with moderate acidity; suffers in compacted or alkaline soils.

Bermudagrass (Cynodon spp.)

Characteristics: Fine to medium texture; dense, vigorous growth; spreads by stolons and rhizomes.

Adaptation: Extremely drought and traffic tolerant; thrives in 25–35 inch rainfall zones and full sun【312468846210205†L34-L68】.

Mowing & water: Mow at 1–2 inches【430033844126791†L70-L126】 and irrigate deeply but infrequently. Requires full sun; shade reduces vigor.

Soil preferences: Adapted to a wide range of soils but prefers well-drained, slightly acidic loam. Overwatering or poor drainage increases disease risk.

Zoysiagrass (Zoysia japonica and Zoysia matrella)

Characteristics: Dense, fine to medium texture; slow growth but forms a thick mat.

Adaptation: Moderate shade and drought tolerance; less nitrogen needed than bermuda【312468846210205†L166-L209】.

Mowing & water: Mow at 1–2.5 inches depending on species【312468846210205†L166-L209】. Water deeply; overwatering can lead to thatch buildup.

Soil preferences: Prefers well-drained soils; tolerates a range of textures but grows slowly in compacted clay.

Buffalograss (Bouteloua dactyloides)

Characteristics: Fine, blue-green blades; forms a low, open turf with natural look.

Adaptation: Native to the Great Plains; well adapted to low rainfall (25 inches or less) and full sun; performs best in Central to West Texas【312468846210205†L73-L93】.

Mowing & water: Requires little irrigation; mow at 2.5–3.5 inches【430033844126791†L70-L126】 or leave unmown for a prairie appearance. Excessive fertilization or irrigation encourages weeds.

Soil preferences: Thrives in well-drained, calcareous soils; poorly adapted to shade or waterlogged conditions.

Other Grasses

Centipedegrass: Low-maintenance turf for acidic soils; not very cold hardy and rarely used in Central Texas.

Hybrid Bermudagrass cultivars (e.g., Tifway, Celebration): Provide improved cold tolerance and disease resistance; managed similarly to common bermuda but may require higher fertility.

Soil Profile Preferences and Management

Healthy turf begins with healthy soil. Central Texas soils range from heavy clays to sandy loams and calcareous rocky soils. To support dense, resilient turf:

  1. Improve soil structure before planting – Incorporate 2–3 inches of compost or other organic matter to a depth of 6–8 inches. This increases water-holding capacity and nutrient availability, particularly in sandy or rocky soils.
  2. Maintain proper pH – Most warm-season grasses prefer a slightly acidic pH (6.0–7.0). Test soil regularly and amend with lime or sulfur as needed.
  3. Provide adequate drainage – Level low spots and install French drains or swales where water stands. Compacted clay soils should be aerated and topdressed with compost annually【255466315847790†L124-L139】.
  4. Avoid over-fertilization – Excess nitrogen encourages lush growth susceptible to disease. Follow soil test recommendations and apply slow-release fertilizers at the correct rate and timing.
  5. Mulch and recycle clippings – Returning clippings supplies nutrients and protects soil from temperature extremes; apply mulch around trees and shrubs to conserve moisture.

Putting It All Together

By following these best management practices—selecting the right turf species for your site, improving soil structure, mowing at the correct height, watering deeply and infrequently, and addressing problems like dry spots, compaction and diseases promptly—you can maintain a healthy, resilient lawn in Central Texas.
